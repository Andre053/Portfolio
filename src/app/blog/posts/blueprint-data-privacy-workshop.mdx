## Context and definitions

**What is sensitive data?**

Sensitive data consists of personal information that can reveal facts about ourselves
	- Ex. demographic data, government-issued identifiers, records, history, personal views, etc. 

**How do we protect it?**

Data privacy focuses on the proper use and governance of personal data
- Policies in place for sensitive data that is collected, shared, and used appropriately
- Governs how personal data is access, disclosed, and managed

Cybersecurity refers to the safeguards an organization implements to protect systems and personal information.
- Data security fits under the cybersecurity umbrella

Data privacy is not cybersecurity
- Data security is a prerequisite to data privacy

**Why is data privacy important?**

1. Legal and regulatory context
	- Regulations to protect data
	- The more data, the more enforcement
2. Responsibility and reputational context
	- People we serve trust us, must handle with care
	- Data can be used to cause harm

## Legal context

**Federally**

- Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA)
	- Federal privacy law that applies to any organization that collects, uses, or discloses personal information
- Canada's Anti-Spam Legislation (CASL)
	- Consent to send emails to participants
- Guidance for research involving humans (not a law)
	- Tri-Council Policy Statement
		- Balance the benefits of research with the protection of participants

**Provincially**

- Often similar to PIPEDA or is a health information law
- Must comply with all laws
- Ontario has the Personal Health Information Protection Act (PHIPA)
	- Health information law

## PIPEDA's 10 principles

1. Accountability: Compliance with data privacy principles
2. Identifying purposes: Collection of data must meet a purpose
3. Consent: Individual must know the data being used for
4. Limiting collection: Only collect data that is needed for the purpose; collection through fair and lawful means
5. Limiting use, disclosure, and retention
6. Accuracy: Personal information must be up-to-date
7. Safeguards: Cybersecurity measures in place
8. Openness: Detailed information about its policies and practices must be public
9. Individual access: Individual must be informed of the existence, use, and disclosure of their personal information on request
10. Challenging compliance: An individual can challenge an organization's compliance with the above principles; their challenge should be addressed

## Common challenges implementing data privacy

1. Data privacy feels overwhelming
	- Limited resources and competing priorities
2. Our data privacy policies are unclear
	- Policies often inconsistently known or not well communicated across the organization
	- Difficult for staff to apply them in practice and for clients to understand how their data is being used
3. Data privacy feels never ending
	- Is a constant challenge

## How to strengthen data privacy

1. Establish an enabling environment
	- Develop a structured framework integrating data privacy
2. Assess your current state
	- What is in place
	- What is missing
3. Sustain data privacy
	- Determine ways you can embed data privacy
	- Cannot treat as a one-off task

## Establishing an enabling environment

Guiding approach: Start small and build momentum
- Find manageable, iterative steps

**Recommendation: Approach data privacy like a project or program to establish an enabling environment**
- Enables you to
	- Assign clear accountability and ownership; ensuring work is sustained over time and competing priorities
	- Track progress and measure outcomes against defined goals
	- Coordinate cross-functional efforts intentionally across teams
- Where to start?
	1. Assign clear accountability
	2. Define scope, timelines, and responsibilities

## Assess your current state

Guiding approach: Familiarize yourself with your data landscape

**Recommendations**
2A. Review organization's privacy documents
* Privacy documents define privacy practices
* Where to start?

2B. Review and establish key data procedures
* Data procedures provide a shared understanding for managing decisions or incidents that could challenge your data privacy or security
* Where to start?
	* Create a list of key procedures relating to sensitive data
	* Review each procedure for accuracy and alignment with privacy documentation
		* Consider making a User Story Map
	* For procedures not documented, write actions that should take place
	* Ensure procedures are clear and accessible to staff
2C. Manage participant and program data
* Key internal processes standardize how participant and program data is collected and managed
	* Identify what data is needed for program delivery
	* Minimize risk by collecting data limited only to what you need
	* Ensure you know what type of sensitive data you are collecting, storing, and who has access to it
	* Strengthen trust with participants by handling their information responsibly
* Where to start?
	* Create a data inventory to document the who, what, when, where, why, and how of data being collected
	* Use it to determine what sensitive information you collect, who has access, and where it is stored
2D. Review physical and technological safeguards
- Safeguards designed to protect data and systems from threats, unauthorized access or damage
	- Reduce risk of system failures and data harm by identifying vulnerabilities and mitigating them
	- Strengthen overall resilience by maintaining proactive, consistent protections that adapt to evolving threats
- Where to start?
	- Create a list of key safeguards in place and desired
		- Physical measures
		- Technological tools
		- Organizational controls
	- Review each process to identify how often each has to be reviewed and the knowledge has to be distributed to staff

## Sustain data privacy

Guiding approach: Maintain check-ins and build a data culture. Foster a culture of shared responsibility to sustain momentum

**Recommendations**

3A. Implement training strategically
- Data privacy and security is a job for all
	- Equalize knowledge across staff of data privacy practices, clarify roles and responsibilities
	- Give staff tools to remain compliant
	- Create shared ownership over data privacy within the organization
- Where to start?
	- Implement a short data privacy and security training session as part of staff onboarding
	- Encourage or require a password manager and require MFA on all platforms
	- Provide refresher privacy and security training on an annual cadence or as part of change management
	- Create a space that is accessible to all staff that contains all privacy documentation and plain language resources

3B. Build a strong data culture
- Embeds data privacy into everyday work
	- Strengthens ongoing governance
	- Builds staff skills and confidence
	- Encourage consistent, proactive identification and mitigation of privacy risks
	- Provide opportunities for staff to engage in policy and procedure review
- Where to start?
	- Incorporate privacy considerations into daily work
	- Make examples out of things that have gone wrong to encourage reporting, transparency, and humility
	- Schedule periodic reviews of policies and procedures to ensure they align in reality

## Case studies: When data privacy and security go wrong

**TPL Cyberattack**

- October 2023
- TPL noticed suspicious activity and found that someone had stolen files affecting more than 4,000 people
- Immediately responded by activating the Major Cyber Security Incident Playbook and Privacy Breach Protocol
	- Contain incident within 24 hours
- Key findings
	- Reported to IPC
	- TPL had legacy software, out-of-date software
	- Threat had sensitive access for more than 2 months
	- TPL provided direct and indirect notice to all affected parties

**LifeLabs Data Breach**

- October 2019
- Exposed data of 8 to 16 million people
- Responded immediately by engaging cyber security experts
- Key findings
	- Ongoing access for almost a year
	- Had administrative account access
	- Ransom was paid
	- Multiple layers of weakness
		- Lack of safeguarding for vulnerabilities
		- Logging and monitoring of privileged accounts were insufficient and the systems did not operate under a least privilege model
		- More data than what was required was collected; did not have file integrity monitoring or retention policies

## What we can learn

- The way you respond to a data breach matters
	- Having processes in place makes it easier 
	- TPL had a good response, no fines
	- LifeLabs was fined
- There are certain actions everyone can do
	- Have processes in place
	- Enforce strong IAM practices
	- Monitor your data and software for unusual activity
	- Notify individuals of what happened
	- Minimize data collected
- Tips
	1. It is always good to start somewhere
	2. Stay focused on your priority areas and celebrate small wins
	3. Try and make things short and simple where possible
	4. Build a habit to promote consistent progress
	5. Lean into project management techniques to support the work
	6. Be patient, building data privacy and capacity takes time
