## What is SDR?
Software-defined radio (SDR) is a radio system that uses a basic hardware 
device along with software implementation of the functionality. While classic
radio systems were implemented fully in hardware, SDR systems only need to have 
hardware to send/receive signals, while all the processing can be done in software. 

SDR gains in flexibility and affordability due to the software-defined nature and 
the simpler engineering of the hardware. Additionally, most software systems are 
open-source and free to use.

## Radio basics and concepts
At a high level, radio systems work like this:
1. A signal is modulated and transmitted at a specific frequency
2. The radio antenna receives the signal on that frequency
3. An analog-to-digital convertor (ADC) samples the analog signal into a digital representation
4. Processing is done to tune to that specific signal, demodulate, and resample it for use
5. To transmit, a digital signal is created and made analog with a digital-to-analog convertor (DAC)
6. The digital signal is transmitted on the frequency

**Modulation**
Modulation is the process of encoding a digital signal. Demodulation decodes it. 

Signals are created using two components: data and a carrier wave. The data must be
encoded as values over time. The carrier wave is set for the specific frequency that
will be transmitting the signal. The data is modulated such that it is part of the 
carrier wave, and the data can be demodulated from the carrier wave. A comparison could

**Gain**
Applying gain to a signal multiplies its strength. 

Gain is typically measured in decibels (dB), a logarithmic unit measuring the intensity
of a signal. A 10x increase in the gain of a signal'samples power is equivalent to a
+10 dB gain.

When a signal has too much power, software/hardware may not be able to output it. 

**Filters**
Applying a filter to a signal aims to reduce the gain of some frequency, while retaining
the original signal for the rest. 

Filters are characterized by the following:
- Passthrough
- Cutoff
- Transition

There are four key types of filters:
- Low
- High
- Band
- Notch

Filtering effectiveness drops in the transition zone of any filter, especially closer
to the cutoff. The underlying algorithm can be improved at the cost of compute. 

**Equalizer**
A system using filters to customize the gain of different bands of frequencies in a 
signal.

A simple equalizer could have 3 filters for the trebel, middle, and bass, respectively. 
Each of these filters can be dialed to apply a different gain to the respective band. 

**Time domain**
The time domain of a signal shows how it changes in power over time. The signal is 
equal to the sum of all frequencies that make up the signal. 

**Frequency domain**
The frequency domain shows the specific frequencies that make up the signal. To view the
frequency domain, a discrete fourier transform must be calculated for the signal. 

**Float and complex representation**
Sound waves can typically be represented using float values.

Radio signals typically are represented by complex values called phasors.
